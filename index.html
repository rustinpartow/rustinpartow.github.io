About me

I am a doctoral candidate at the UCLA Economics Department. I am a labor economist with an emphasis on dynamic interactions between workers and firms. I am on the market in 2019/20.

Job Market Paper
    Coming Soon!

    This paper uses novel longitudinal data to show that the careers of US lawyers tend to exhibit mobility down the industry heirarchy. I argue that downward mobility is a symptom of an assymetric information problem caused by private employer learning. I present a model of dynamic labor market matching with private learning and two-sided heterogeneity. Private learning creates lopsided mobility, in which low-ranked firms frequently poach lawyers from high-ranked firms, but not vice-versa. The reason is that, in the event that a low-ranked firm acquires a star employee, there is no mechanism by which the high-ranked firms can identify her. For star employees, the gains of working at high-ranked firms are very large, but the only way to arrive at a high-ranked firm is to start there. To avoid missing out on a chance for super-star earnings, lawyers are forced to target initial over-placement, relative to a setting with full information.
